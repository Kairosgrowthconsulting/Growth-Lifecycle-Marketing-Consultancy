When CAC rises and conversion rates drop, most brands double down on acquisition. More budget, more creative tests, more desperation.

Here's why that's backwards.

## The Unit Economics Problem

Acquisition math breaks in two places:

1. **Rising CAC**: You're bidding against more competitors for the same inventory
2. **Falling LTV**: New customers convert at lower rates and retain worse

When both happen at once, the cohort economics fall apart. You're paying $80 to acquire a customer worth $60.

## Why Lifecycle Wins

Your existing customer file doesn't have this problem:

- **Zero acquisition cost**: You already paid to acquire them
- **Known behavior**: You have actual purchase data, not proxy signals
- **Warm relationship**: They've already bought once

A well-structured lifecycle program can generate 30-40% of revenue from the existing file.

## What Actually Works

Three lifecycle mechanics that work regardless of market conditions:

### 1. Post-Purchase Series
Set up a 14-day series after first purchase:
- Day 1: Order confirmation + education
- Day 3: How to get the most value
- Day 7: Cross-sell based on first purchase
- Day 14: Replenishment reminder

### 2. Win-Back Sequences
Segment by days since last purchase:
- 60-90 days: Gentle nudge
- 90-120 days: Incentive offer
- 120+ days: Last-chance campaign

### 3. Value-Based Cohorts
Separate treatment for:
- High LTV customers (VIP treatment)
- Mid LTV customers (consistent engagement)
- Low LTV customers (efficient automation)

## The Bottom Line

If you have 10,000 customers and you move the needle on repurchase rate by just 5%, that's 500 additional orders without spending a dollar on acquisition.

Lifecycle isn't a nice-to-have channel. In tough markets, it's often the only channel with positive unit economics.
